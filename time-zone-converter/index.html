How to convert time zones manually

  1. Find each zone's current UTC offset (varies with daylight saving).
  2. Subtract source offset from the source time to get UTC.
  3. Add target offset to UTC to get the target time.

Example: 9:00 AM in New York (UTC−4 in summer) → 13:00 UTC → 15:00 in Berlin (UTC+2 in summer).

Quick examples

  • 9:00 AM New York → 15:00 Berlin (summer)
  • 9:00 AM New York → 22:00 Tokyo (same day)
  • 5:00 PM London → 9:00 AM Los Angeles (winter)
  • 10:00 AM Sydney → 00:00 UTC (midnight)
  • Noon UTC → 1:00 PM London (summer) / Noon (winter)

Formulas used

UTC = source time − source offset

target time = UTC + target offset

(uses browser Intl API for IANA database)

Eight major zones are included: UTC, US Eastern, US Pacific, London, Central Europe, Dubai, Tokyo, and Sydney. They cover the bulk of business time-zone questions for North America, Europe, Middle East, and Asia-Pacific. Daylight saving is handled automatically.

Common uses

  • Scheduling meetings with international colleagues
  • Joining a webinar or live event in another country
  • Calling family abroad without waking them up
  • Booking flights and figuring out arrival time at destination
  • Coordinating remote work across continents

Frequently asked questions

Does it handle daylight saving time?

Yes. The browser's Intl API knows when each zone switches between standard and summer time, so the conversion is correct on any date in the current year. The calculation always uses today's date.

Why isn't my city in the list?

We kept the list to eight major zones to stay simple. Most cities share a zone with a listed one (e.g., Berlin = Prague, Paris = Prague, Hong Kong ≈ Tokyo + 1h, Mumbai = Dubai + 1.5h). For full city coverage you can use a dedicated tool like timeanddate.com.

How do I figure out a meeting time across three zones?

Pick the source (your zone) and run the conversion to each target separately. There's no three-way view here — that's a different kind of tool, often called a "world clock" or "team scheduler."

Why does the same time give a different result tomorrow?

Because the conversion uses today's date. If a daylight saving change happens between today and the target date, the offset changes by one hour. For dates far in the future, double-check around DST switch days (March and November in most places).
